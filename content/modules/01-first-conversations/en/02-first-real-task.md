## Hook

Saying hi was nice. Now let's make AI actually useful: pick one real, small task from *your* week — an email you're avoiding, homework you're stuck on, a dinner you haven't planned — and get it done in the next ten minutes.

## Core idea

AI works dramatically better on **concrete tasks** than on vague requests. The difference between a useless answer and a great one is usually in your prompt, not in the AI.

A concrete prompt has three parts:

1. **What you want** — "Write a short email", "Explain this concept", "Give me 3 dinner ideas".
2. **The details that matter** — who it's for, what's in the fridge, which grade level.
3. **What "good" looks like** — "friendly but short", "explain like I'm 12", "under 30 minutes to cook".

You don't need magic words. You need the same details you'd give a helpful human.

> **For younger learners:** Asking AI "help me with homework" is like telling a friend "help me with school." Which subject? Which question? What have you tried? The more you tell, the better the help.

## Show me

Same task, two prompts:

**Vague:** "Give me a dinner idea."
→ *"How about spaghetti bolognese? It's a classic crowd-pleaser..."* (generic, ignores your reality)

**Specific:** "Give me 3 dinner ideas for tonight. Constraints: vegetarian, 30 minutes max, must use the zucchini and eggs in my fridge, and one of the eaters is a picky 8-year-old."
→ *"1. Zucchini fritters with yogurt dip — crispy enough for picky eaters... 2. Shakshuka with grated zucchini hidden in the sauce... 3. Egg-fried rice with zucchini coins..."* (usable tonight)

Same AI. The details did the work.

## Your turn

Pick one real task from your actual week. Some starters:

- **Mode A — Go try it:** Open your AI tool and do the task for real: draft that email, plan that meal, get unstuck on that homework question. Iterate at least once ("shorter", "friendlier", "explain that step again").
- **Mode B — Copy this prompt:** "I need help with a real task: [describe your task]. Important details: [who it's for / constraints / deadline]. A good result looks like: [tone, length, format]. Ask me up to 2 clarifying questions before answering."
- **Mode C — Try it here:** Describe your task in the chat box with all three parts: what, details, what good looks like.

Don't accept the first answer. Reply with one improvement request — that's where the magic happens.

## Wrap-up

Concrete beats vague: say what you want, give the details that matter, describe what good looks like — then iterate.

**Reflection:** Was the first answer or the improved second answer better? What detail made the difference?
