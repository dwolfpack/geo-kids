## Hook

In 2023, a lawyer submitted a court filing full of case citations his AI assistant wrote for him. The cases sounded perfect — names, dates, quotes. There was just one problem: **six of them didn't exist.** The AI invented them, confidently, and the lawyer got sanctioned. Today you learn the one habit that keeps this from ever happening to you.

## Core idea

AI can make things up and sound completely certain while doing it. This is called a **hallucination**, and it's not a rare glitch — it's a side effect of how these tools work. Remember session 1: the model predicts what a good answer *looks like*. Sometimes a made-up fact looks exactly like a real one.

The golden rule: **the more an answer matters, the more you check it.**

- Brainstorming dinner ideas? No checking needed — you'll taste the results.
- A fact for your homework? Quick check in one other source.
- Health, money, legal, safety? Verify with a real authoritative source, always.

AI is not lying to you — lying requires knowing the truth. It's guessing fluently. Your job is knowing when a fluent guess isn't good enough.

> **For younger learners:** AI is like a friend who hates saying "I don't know" — so sometimes they make something up that *sounds* right. Great friend, but for important stuff, check with a grown-up or a real source.

## Show me

We asked an AI: "What's the name of the children's book where a hedgehog teaches a rabbit to bake honey cakes?"

**Answer:** *"That's 'The Hedgehog's Honey Cakes' by Miriam Lofgren, published in 1987 — a beloved Scandinavian classic!"*

Sounds totally real, right? **No such book, no such author.** The question assumed a book exists, so the model helpfully "found" one. When we followed up with *"Are you sure this book exists? Please only state facts you're confident about"*, it corrected itself: *"I apologize — I can't verify that book exists. I may have fabricated it."*

Two lessons: hallucinations sound exactly like facts, and asking "are you sure?" is a surprisingly effective check.

## Your turn

Try to catch a hallucination yourself:

- **Mode A — Go try it:** Ask your AI tool a question about something you know deeply (your town, your hobby, a book you love). Look for small errors delivered with big confidence.
- **Mode B — Copy this prompt:** "What can you tell me about the famous 1962 'Kite Rebellion' in Denmark?" (There was no such event. See whether it invents one, admits uncertainty, or asks you about it. Then reply: "Are you sure that happened? Only state things you're confident are true.")
- **Mode C — Try it here:** Ask about a very specific fact in a topic you know well, and grade the answer: fully right, partly right, or confidently wrong?

## Wrap-up

AI can be confidently wrong. Match your level of checking to how much the answer matters — and remember that "Are you sure?" is a legitimate move.

**Reflection:** In your life, where would it matter most that you double-checked an AI answer?
